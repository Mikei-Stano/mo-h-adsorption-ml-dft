Timeouts and the State of the Art for GPAW Screening of HER on Mo Materials
What Your Workflow Actually Decides
Your script is a typical high-throughput first-pass screening workflow for HER: it takes many slabs and interfaces, estimates the H binding energy, and ranks candidates according to a simple descriptor. This type of approach is still fully legitimate and is used very frequently in modern research. At the same time, however, it is no longer regarded as a complete picture of catalytic activity. The most prestigious methodological reviews from recent years emphasize that realistic modelling of heterogeneous electrocatalysis must increasingly include solvation, electrolyte ions, electrode potential, kinetics, pH, and reaction networks, rather than only a single adsorption quantity in an ideal vacuum. [1]
This is important also because, although HER still uses ΔG_H as a first filter, the trend of the last three years is clear: ΔG_H remains useful as a screening descriptor, not as a stand-alone final truth about activity. Large data pipelines for HER still use hydrogen adsorption free energy as the core of models and databases, including work on 10,855 HER catalysts from Catalysis-Hub and descriptor studies for MXenes; therefore, your direction is methodologically well grounded. [2]
In your specific code, it is also important to name the calculation correctly: what you are calculating today is a screening-level energetic proxy for HER, not a fully realistic electrochemical free energy under operando conditions. This does not mean that it is wrong. It means that the result is suitable for triage and prioritization of candidates, not yet for a final publishable mechanistic interpretation. This shift from “single-number screening” to “screening + refinement” is exactly where the state of the art has moved. [3]
Where the State of the Art in HER Modelling Has Moved
The strongest shift in recent literature is that hydrogen binding energy alone is no longer always sufficient even conceptually. A 2025 paper in Angewandte shows that, for single-atom catalysts, H binding can be misleading because activity is also fundamentally affected by HO* or O* poisoning; the authors therefore propose a combined descriptor, ΔG_H* and ΔG_HO*. A 2026 paper in ACS Catalysis goes even further by introducing the charge state of adsorbed H* as a missing kinetic descriptor and recommends a two-descriptor approach rather than a single thermodynamic metric. [4]
A similar trend can also be seen outside single-atom catalysis. In a 2025 Chemical Science study on 2D carbide MXenes, the authors show that ΔG_H alone does not capture the internal structural characteristics and that better screening emerges when the descriptor is constructed in a mechanism-guided manner, that is, from structural and electronic factors simultaneously. In other words, the field is not moving toward “discarding ΔG_H,” but toward “retaining ΔG_H while embedding it in mechanistic context and complementing it with a second descriptor.” [5]
For your project, this implies a very practical point. If you want to rapidly sift through MoB, MoS2, MoSe2, Mo2N, Mo2C, and Ni–Mo interfaces, your current descriptor makes sense. However, if you later want to make stronger claims such as “this structure is the best HER-active phase,” you will need to supplement the top candidates with at least part of what the literature currently considers decisive: the microenvironment, OH/H2O steps, and possibly a simple kinetic or two-descriptor refinement. [6]
What the Latest Studies Say About MoS2, Mo2C, and Mo Interfaces
If we look specifically at the Mo family, the best recent studies focus mainly on MoS2, Mo2C, and MoOxCy, and somewhat less directly on pure MoB. This is an important detail: for your MoB and Ni_MoB_interface systems, it is worth also reading the literature on chemically related Mo carbides and Mo chalcogenides, because the mechanistic lessons are highly transferable. For MoB itself, however, there is already a new top-tier signal: a 2026 paper in Advanced Energy Materials shows that molybdenum-boride interfaces are moving to the centre of HER design through interfacial nanocluster design and modification of the hydrogen-spillover pathway. [7]
For MoS2, the current consensus is very strong. A recent review in Chemical Science emphasizes that the bulk basal plane is intrinsically weak, whereas HER activity is carried by edge sites and electronically unsaturated centres; therefore, the field focuses on edge exposure, defects, phase engineering, doping, and interlayer coupling. This is consistent with a 2024 review in ACS Catalysis, which summarizes heteroatom doping as a major tool for activating MoS2 for HER. For your generation of candidate H positions, this means that static “top/bridge/centroid” probing is a reasonable beginning, but for the best candidates it must not be the end. [8]
For Mo2C and MoOxCy, recent results are even more instructive for both your timeout question and the mechanism. Nature Communications showed in 2025 that oxide/oxycarbide terminations and the local acidity of the termination layer fundamentally change alkaline HER because they improve water adsorption and activation and generate reversible HxMoyOz-like intermediates. Another paper in the same year showed that carbonization of MoO2 into MoOxCy weakens the Mo–H bond and enables operation up to industrial current densities of 10 A cm^-2. This is a very strong message: in Mo systems, what matters is not only “how strongly a clean surface binds H,” but also what termination, oxygen chemistry, and interfacial environment actually emerge during operation. [9]
For Ni–Mo interfaces and alkaline media, today’s literature is even more unequivocal. Nature Communications showed in 2025, on a NiMoO4-based system, that alkaline HER performance is fundamentally changed by the structure of the electric double layer, the local hydrogen-bonding network, and a dynamically formed epitaxial hydroxide layer; thus, the issue is not only the thermodynamic barrier for H adsorption/desorption on a “dry” surface. At the same time, a 2024 review in Energy & Environmental Science summarizes that active sites dynamically transform during photo- and electrocatalysis, and that these changes often govern adsorption, activation, and deactivation. For your Ni_MoB_interface_* systems, this means that the most interesting structures may be precisely those that run longer because they reconstruct, hydroxylate, or switch local bonding motifs. [10]
What the Literature Implies for Your GPAW Settings
At the level of computational philosophy, your intuition is very good. A 2024 review of GPAW explicitly states that LCAO is less accurate than PW/FD, but allows significantly larger systems, and that a sensible strategy is to first perform a fast LCAO optimization pass and then carry out refinement in PW or FD. This corresponds exactly to what you want to do: fast screening followed by a more detailed rerun of the best candidates. [11]
At the same time, however, the GPAW documentation points out two major methodological prices that you pay for this. The first is that single-zeta is a minimal basis, that is, an aggressively inexpensive basis. The second is that, in LCAO adsorption calculations, the adsorbate can “benefit” from the basis functions of the surface, which leads to basis set superposition error and typically to excessively strong binding. Therefore, basis=sz is excellent for speed, but its physical cost must be acknowledged: it is a tool for coarse screening, not for final adsorption energetics. [12]
This is exactly why I would regard your combination of basis=sz, kpts=2,2,1, relax-steps=3, and fmax=0.15 as methodologically defensible as Stage 1, but not as a final “production truth” in the publication-level sense. Recent reviews on errors in computational electrocatalysis show that even with common GGA functionals, errors propagate into adsorption energies, volcano plots, and screening decisions; modern methodological reviews therefore focus the discussion on the GGA family, such as PBE, RPBE, and BEEF-vdW, and on their systematic errors. A fairly hard conclusion follows from this: if you go even lower to LDA + sz + sparse k-points + short relaxation, you must explicitly understand it as a ranking engine, not as a quantitative electrocatalytic method. [13]
Another important detail is performance. The GPAW documentation states that, in LCAO, matrix operations scale with the number of orbitals and that ScaLAPACK is particularly important for larger LCAO calculations, becoming worthwhile at roughly around 800 orbitals; at the same time, ELPA is also recommended. This is exactly the type of bottleneck you should expect for MoB/Ni–Mo interface slabs. However, your current execution model launches ordinary Python and distributes parallelism through ProcessPoolExecutor, so an individual GPAW job probably runs as one MPI rank + 8 BLAS/OpenMP threads, not as a genuinely MPI-parallel GPAW calculation. In other words, a larger part of the potential speedup lies in the execution model and GPAW’s internal parallelization rather than in timeouts. [14]
Your adsorption geometry is also a reasonable compromise, but not the state-of-the-art final stage. New studies on MoS2 and HER in general show that activity is highly sensitive to site diversity, edges, defects, dual sites, and reconstructed interfaces. Your candidate generator is better than a single top site, but for the top candidates after screening I would definitely expand the site search or perform a second pass with richer local H initialization, and possibly also with OH/H2O-active motifs for alkaline media. [15]
Timeouts, Checkpoints, and Workflow Robustness
Directly to your question: I do not consider making a per-structure timeout the main control mechanism of a production DFT/HT workflow to be state of the art. A modern robust workflow is instead based on checkpoint/restart, continuous saving, resumability, and modular orchestration. GPAW can write .gpw restart files and can even append them after every n-th SCF step so that an interrupted expensive calculation can be resumed from an intermediate state. ASE supports trajectories and Hessian restarts for BFGS/LBFGS. And today’s workflow frameworks in materials science are built precisely for programmable, restartable, fault-tolerant pipelines. [16]
Conversely, a per-structure timeout makes very good sense during calibration and benchmarking. GPAW documentation itself recommends experimenting with the MPI/OpenMP ratio according to the hardware and input before production calculations. When you perform A/B tests such as 4x4x1 vs 2x2x1, 1 worker vs 4 workers, and dzp vs sz, a timeout creates a fair time window and prevents one bad setup from consuming the entire slot. Here I agree with you: for tuning, yes. [17]
In strict production, however, a timeout is only a safeguard against a pathological state, not a solution to the performance problem. I would even say that, for your topic, it can sometimes be counterproductive: recent literature says that in HER, especially in alkaline environments and on Mo/Ni interfaces, the microenvironment, reconstruction, and interfacial chemistry are precisely what matter. This means that “difficult” structures that run for a long time are not necessarily bad structures; they may be chemically interesting structures. This is an inference from the methodological and mechanistic literature, not explicitly a scheduler paper, but I consider it very strong. [18]
Therefore, in production I would prefer the following hierarchy: the global walltime of the batch; checkpoint/restart for SCF and relaxations; continuous CSV/JSON writing; and only then a very loose watchdog timeout, set so high that it catches deadlocks or extreme outliers rather than normal slow convergence. In your case, you already have crash-safe CSV output and graceful shutdown, so the next natural step is not a timeout but the addition of GPAW and ASE restart mechanisms. [16]
Verdict for Your Production Run
My concise answer is: yes to a timeout for calibration, but not as the main tool for production. This is not a polite compromise, but a fairly accurate reflection of where leading work has moved over the last three years: screening is done quickly and at multiple levels, but robustness is handled through restarts and workflow orchestration, not by “shooting down” slow structures. [16]
For your specific node1 production run, I would calmly disable the timeout, or leave only a very large safeguard if you add checkpointing. The reason is simple: the larger performance problem is probably that your current model uses coarse-fidelity screening parameters while at the same time not fully exploiting GPAW’s internal parallelization for large LCAO matrices. In other words: the timeout is not the root of the problem; the execution model, basis, site search, and absence of restart mechanisms are more important. [19]
Scientifically, I would describe your current stack as a good Stage 1 pipeline, not as a final HER pipeline. For MoB and Ni_MoB_interface_*, it is fully usable for the first ranking of candidates. However, I would subsequently rerun the top results with higher fidelity, a richer site search, and at least partially interface-aware/alkaline-aware interpretation, because that is exactly where today’s literature locates the difference between “fast screening” and the true state of the art. [6]
If I should put it in one sentence: your instinct is correct — timeouts are a good safeguard for tuning, but a poor main solution for production GPAW screening of HER on Mo interfaces. The modern approach is: fast first-pass screening, robust checkpoints, and then higher fidelity only for the winners. [20]
